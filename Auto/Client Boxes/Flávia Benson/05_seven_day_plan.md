**Why this move:**

- *"I owe you a real apology for missing the 10 AM call on Friday. That was on me. I set the time and didn't honor it."* — **owns it cleanly, three short sentences, no excuses, no qualifications.** Compliance attorneys live in a world where excuses are red flags. *"That was on me. - *"Here's how we fix it"* — **forward motion.** Names that there's a remedy and we're proposing it. **Compliance work is fundamentally about remediation processes** — naming the fix the way she would name it is the move that demonstrates we understand her world.
- **One specific time proposed: Sunday 11 AM.** Not three options. **One.** Multiple-choice slots in this message would dilute the accountability message. *We chose a time, we're committing to it, here's the time.* If it doesn't work for her, the next sentence gives her the lane to redirect. **But the default is one clean commitment.**
- *"I'll be on time. If for any reason you don't see my call by 11:01, you can call me at this number. That's the commitment."* — **dual accountability.** She doesn't have to wonder whether we'll show up; she has the number and the permission to call us if we don't. **For a compliance professional, the structure of "here's the backup if I fail again" is exactly the language of well-designed risk mitigation.** This sentence alone might be the difference between her giving us a second chance and writing us off.
- *"If 11 AM Sunday doesn't work, send me one specific slot that does, and I'll hold to that one instead."* — **gives her control without giving her vagueness.** Note the language: *"one specific slot."* Not "let me know what works." *"One specific slot."* That's the dialect she uses; we're using it back to her.
- **Around 80 words.** Tight. No filler. No pleasantries. **The brevity is itself part of the message.** *"This is a serious recovery from a serious miss; we're not going to pad it with unnecessary words."*
- **No emoji. Capitalized. Properly punctuated.** Same register the message demands.
